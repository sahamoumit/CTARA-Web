Non-woven Silk (Jivodaya Silk) Fabric Making and Forward Linkages

PI: Prof. Amit Arora / Prof. Satish Agnihotri, C-TARA
Inventor: Mr. Sashwat Ghai, C-TARA, IIT Bombay

The traditional silk harvesting process involves boiling cocoons, killing silkworms and limiting farmers' earnings to approximately Rs. 1-1.4 per cocoon (Rs. 400-650 per kg). There is a need for a more ethical and profitable approach at the farmers' site, preserving silkworms and offering higher income potential through a value-added product.

Sustainable Silk Production and Farmer Empowerment

Material Innovation

Develop Jivodaya silk fabric that prevents the killing of silkworms, promoting conscious consumption.

Technology Implementation

Conduct field testing of the flattening equipment in sericulture clusters to evaluate its practicality and effectiveness.

Farmer Empowerment

Enhance small and marginal farmers' income by providing access to flattening technology, improving Quality of Life (QoL) through better earnings.

Collaboration and Linkages

Build sustainable sericulture communities by establishing forward linkages to the art industry and market, and backward linkages to educational institutes and NGOs.

Salient features: What Sets It Apart
  • Silk Production: Harvests silk without harming silkworms, promoting ahimsa (peace) silk and conscious consumption.
  • Eco-friendly and Cost-Effective: Operates without fuel or electricity, offering a sustainable solution for rural farmers.
  • User-friendly Technology: Designed with simple, familiar tools, ensuring ease of use and low maintenance.
  • Additional Income Source: Empowers small and marginal farmers by creating a complementary income stream alongside traditional sericulture.

RuTAG IITB Intervention

Rotatory Jig for Uniform Silk Fabric Production

The rotatory jig is a specialized equipment measuring 2440 mm long, 1066 mm wide, and 1219 mm high. It features a bearing at the end connected to a stepper motor and a timer. The motor rotates slowly at 0.1 rpm, allowing even silk deposition by adjusting the Z-axis movement. This controlled movement helps in creating uniform non-woven silk fabric.

Environmental Control for Better Silk Quality

To ensure high-quality silk production, the temperature and relative humidity are maintained using passive techniques. These methods protect the silkworms from external environmental changes, ensuring stable and favorable conditions for silk formation. By controlling these factors, the silk deposition remains consistent and high-quality.

Training Farmers for Effective Implementation

RuTAG provides a two-step training program for farmers. Initially, they learn the process using a flat plate on a small scale. Once they gain experience, they advance to using the rotating jig. This training approach ensures efficient skill development and helps farmers adopt the new technology effectively.

Adoption & Impact
  • The Jivodaya silk technology is currently in use at BAIF, Uruli Kanchan, Pune, where the silk is transformed into unique art pieces
  • Ongoing efforts to establish additional demonstration sites with BAIF and other agencies
  • The technology’s adaptability can be tested in diverse Indian sericulture regions
  • Collaboration opportunities with the Central Silk Board and its affiliated institutes, such as CMER&TI in Assam
  • Engagement with state agencies like the Directorate of Textiles, Handlooms, and Handicrafts in Odisha and Maharashtra to expand adoption
